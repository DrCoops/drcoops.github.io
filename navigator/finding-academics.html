⚠️ Watch out for these common pitfalls

  • Going back to the same people: It is very easy to keep engaging with the same academics. Actively using the methods below helps diversify your network and brings in fresh perspectives.
  • Only using your own networks: Your existing contacts are a starting point, not an endpoint. Brokers like UPEN and the Academies can open doors you didn't know existed.
  • Unclear asks: Academics respond much better when the ask is clear — what you need, when you need it, and what you will do with their input.
  • Not engaging early enough: Many forms of engagement (secondments, commissioning, student projects) need months of lead time. Start earlier than you think.

Methods for finding academics

Each method has different timelines and works best at different stages of your engagement journey

📱

Social media

Timeline: months to build; hours once established

LinkedIn and X/Twitter are both effective for discovering and engaging academic experts, with non-overlapping communities: more engineering, business and finance on LinkedIn; more social, physical sciences and economics on X. Key tactics include making lists in X against particular topical areas, and posting questions and ideas on LinkedIn to draw in respondents. Requires low-level, longer-term investment but the process of building your list also builds your knowledge of the topic.

📧

Academic mailservers / Jiscmail

Timeline: days to subscribe; can reach community quickly

Academics use semi-centralised newsletter distribution servers (listservs) to share news and discuss topics in their field. The main UK network is Jiscmail. Once subscribed you can follow discussions in your domain and post requests to the community. Topics covered are non-systematic — there may not be a relevant list, or there may be several. A useful way of following developments in your area and engaging a wide range of academics and researchers.

Your local analysts and scientists

Timeline: immediate — they are already in your organisation

Government Social Researchers, economists, statisticians, operational researchers and scientists all maintain networks with academics in their fields. Many work with academics via contracted research, advisory groups and conferences. They are often the quickest and most effective route to finding the right academic — particularly for your specific policy area — and can help you critically appraise what they say.

ARI database

Timeline: instant for published ARIs

The ARI (Areas of Research Interest) database allows you to search not only for ARIs, but connects particular ARIs to UKRI-funded research projects. This allows you to find live projects related to your policy area and the academics who lead them. Very quick where ARIs are already published; much longer (months) where new ARIs are first needed.

🗺️

Expert mapping (database search)

Timeline: hours–days with database access; longer via student/intern

Expert mapping uses academic research databases (Scopus, Web of Science, Google Scholar, Dimensions.AI) to systematically identify who has published on your policy topic, particularly in UK institutions. The technique works like a Rapid Evidence Assessment — you define search terms for your policy area and filter by UK affiliation to map the expertise landscape. Can be done in-house (if you have database access), or as part of a student project, internship, or via direct advice from an academic.

UPEN — University Policy Engagement Network

Timeline: weeks

UPEN is a network of over 100 UK university policy offices covering around two-thirds of all UK universities. Their role is to help connect academic researchers to officials in UK government. You can contact UPEN with a request for expertise on a topic and they will circulate it to member universities. This is a particularly good route for reaching academics who are not already in your network and for finding people in specific disciplines or institutions.

The Academies

Timeline: days to weeks

The four national Academies (Royal Society, British Academy, Academy of Medical Sciences, Royal Academy of Engineering) retain lists of Fellows who are leading academics or practitioners in their fields. You can contact their teams with a request for expertise on a topic and they can quickly suggest relevant fellows. This is particularly good for reaching very senior academics and for finding people across a very wide range of disciplines.

🔗 Royal Society 🔗 British Academy 🔗 Acad. Medical Sciences 🔗 Royal Acad. Engineering
📊

CSA, SAC and ALBs

Timeline: quick — already within your organisational network

Your Chief Scientific Adviser, Science Advisory Committees, and Arms-Length Bodies with scientific capability all have their own academic networks — often complementary to those of your analysts and scientists. The SAC/ALB map tool shows all main SACs and scientific ALBs filtered by research topic, using the same topic words as the ARI database. You can search the ARI database to find relevant projects, then use the research topic tags to filter the map to find internal contacts who can help.
